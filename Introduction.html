Introduction

Semiconductors are materials whose electrical conductivity lies between that of conductors and insulators. Their unique electrical properties can be controlled by changing temperature, adding impurities (doping), or applying electric fields. Because of this controllability, semiconductors form the foundation of modern electronic devices such as diodes, transistors, integrated circuits, solar cells, and communication systems. Among the various semiconductor materials available, Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), and Gallium Arsenide (GaAs) are the most important and widely studied. Each of these materials has different physical and electrical properties, such as band gap energy, carrier mobility, conductivity, and temperature sensitivity. These differences make them suitable for different applications. For example, Silicon is commonly used in integrated circuits due to its stability and low cost, Germanium offers high carrier mobility, and Gallium Arsenide is widely used in high-frequency and optoelectronic applications due to its superior electron mobility and direct band gap. The performance of semiconductor devices strongly depends on material properties such as band gap, carrier mobility, and conductivity. The band gap determines how easily electrons can move from the valence band to the conduction band. Carrier mobility affects how quickly charge carriers move through the material, influencing device speed. Conductivity determines how well the material allows electric current to flow, while temperature behavior affects device reliability and efficiency. This project focuses on comparing Silicon, Germanium, and Gallium Arsenide in terms of their fundamental physical and electrical properties. By analyzing these properties theoretically and through simulation, the project helps in understanding how material selection affects the performance of semiconductor devices and why specific materials are preferred in particular applications.
