Semiconductor Material Comparison

1. Silicon (Si): The Industry Standard
  • Silicon dominates because it forms a high-quality natural oxide (SiO2), which acts as a perfect insulator for transistors.
  • Thermal Robustness: It maintains its properties up to approximately 150°C, making it safe for the heat generated by modern CPUs.
  • The "Indirect" Factor: Because it has an indirect band gap, electrons lose energy as heat rather than light when they drop energy levels, which is why your laptop gets hot but doesn't glow.
2. Germanium (Ge): The Original Pioneer
  • The first transistors were actually Germanium, but its low band gap (Eg ≈0.67 eV) means it starts leaking current at relatively low temperatures.
  • Conductivity: It has higher hole mobility than Silicon, making it useful in certain high-speed switching and "Strained Silicon" technologies to boost performance.
  • Infrared Transparency: It is highly transparent to infrared light, making it the gold standard for thermal imaging lenses and night-vision sensors.
3. Gallium Arsenide (GaAs): The High-Performance Elite
  • Unlike Si and Ge, GaAs is a compound semiconductor. It is more difficult and expensive to manufacture but offers physics that simple elements can't match.
  • Direct Band Gap: This allows for efficient photon emission. If you have a fiber-optic internet connection, the laser at the other end is likely made of GaAs or a related compound.
  • Radiation Hardness: It is much more resistant to cosmic radiation than Silicon, which is why it's the preferred choice for satellite electronics and space-based solar cells.
