The comparative study of semiconductor materials such as Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), and Gallium Arsenide (GaAs) highlights their unique electrical and physical properties that influence their performance in electronic devices. Silicon, with its moderate band gap, high thermal stability, and cost-effective availability, remains the most widely used semiconductor material in the electronics industry. Germanium, due to its higher charge carrier mobility, provides better electrical conductivity but suffers from thermal instability, making it less suitable for high-temperature applications. On the other hand, Gallium Arsenide exhibits superior electron mobility and direct band gap characteristics, enabling high-speed operation and efficient optoelectronic performance. Thus, Silicon is best suited for general-purpose electronic devices, Germanium for specialized high-sensitivity applications, and Gallium Arsenide for high-frequency and optoelectronic devices. The performance of these materials ultimately depends on application requirements such as speed, temperature tolerance, efficiency, and cost.
