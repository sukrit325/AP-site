Introduction to Semiconductor Materials

Semiconductors are materials whose electrical conductivity lies between conductors and insulators. Their conductivity can be controlled by temperature, electric field, and impurity doping. This unique property makes semiconductors essential for electronic devices such as diodes, transistors, solar cells, and integrated circuits.

Band Theory

The electrical behavior of semiconductors is explained using band theory of solids, which describes the energy levels available to electrons.

Valence Band

Occupied by electrons in lower energy states. These are the electrons involved in covalent bonding.

Conduction Band

Where electrons move freely and conduct electricity. Empty or partially filled at room temperature.

Band Gap (Eg)

The energy difference between the valence and conduction bands. Critical for device operation.

Key Process

If electrons gain sufficient energy (thermal or optical), they jump from the valence band to the conduction band, leaving behind holes. Both electrons and holes act as charge carriers, enabling electrical conduction.

Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductors

Pure semiconductors without added impurities

  • Definition: A pure semiconductor with no added impurities
  • Examples: Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge)
  • Electron-Hole Balance: Number of electrons = number of holes
  • Conductivity Control: Depends mainly on temperature
  • Conductivity Level: Lower conductivity at room temperature

Extrinsic Semiconductors

Semiconductors with intentionally added impurities

  • Process: Created through doping (adding impurities)
  • n-type: Excess electrons (donor impurity - Group V elements)
  • p-type: Excess holes (acceptor impurity - Group III elements)
  • Conductivity: Significantly increased through doping
  • Control: Precise control over properties through doping levels

Band Gap Energy

Band gap is the most important property that determines semiconductor performance. It influences thermal stability, optical properties, and overall device reliability.

Band Gap Relationships

Small Band Gap
↓
Higher conductivity, More leakage current
Large Band Gap
↑
Lower leakage, Better high temp performance

Typical Band Gap Values

Material Band Gap (eV) Type Thermal Stability Best Application
Silicon (Si) 1.12 eV Moderate Good Logic ICs, Power Devices
Germanium (Ge) 0.66 eV Smallest Poor Infrared Detectors
Gallium Arsenide (GaAs) 1.43 eV Largest Excellent Optoelectronics, 5G

Material Analysis

  • Germanium: Has the smallest band gap (0.66 eV), so it conducts easily but is unstable at high temperatures due to increased leakage current.
  • Silicon: Moderate band gap (1.12 eV) provides the best balance between conductivity and thermal stability, making it ideal for most applications.
  • Gallium Arsenide: Larger band gap (1.43 eV) makes it suitable for high-frequency and optoelectronic applications with superior thermal performance.

Charge Carrier Mobility

Mobility refers to how fast electrons and holes move when an electric field is applied. It's a critical parameter for determining device speed and frequency response.

Mobility Formula

μ = vd / E

  • μ = Mobility (cm²/V·s)
  • vd = Drift velocity (cm/s)
  • E = Electric field (V/cm)

Impact of Higher Mobility

Faster Switching Devices

Enable higher operational frequencies

Better Performance

High-frequency operation capability

Advanced Applications

Radar, microwave, and 5G systems

Electron Mobility Comparison

Silicon (Si)
1,350 cm²/V·s
Germanium (Ge)
3,900 cm²/V·s
Gallium Arsenide (GaAs)
8,500 cm²/V·s

Key Insight

GaAs has the highest mobility among the three materials, making it ideal for microwave and high-speed applications. This superior mobility enables faster switching speeds and higher operational frequencies compared to Silicon and Germanium.

Electrical Conductivity

Electrical conductivity depends on charge carrier concentration and mobility. These two factors together determine how well a material conducts electricity.

Conductivity Formula

σ = q(nμn + pμp)

  • σ = Conductivity (S/cm)
  • q = Charge of electron (1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C)
  • n = Electron concentration
  • p = Hole concentration
  • μn, μp = Electron and hole mobilities

Factors Affecting Conductivity

Carrier Concentration

Higher number of charge carriers increases conductivity significantly

Carrier Mobility

Faster moving carriers enhance overall conductivity

Temperature

Increased thermal energy increases carrier mobility and concentration

Doping Level

Controlled impurity addition dramatically increases conductivity

Material Conductivity Comparison

Germanium has higher conductivity than Silicon due to smaller band gap, resulting in more intrinsic carriers at room temperature.

Gallium Arsenide also shows high conductivity due to very high electron mobility, despite having a larger band gap than Silicon.

Silicon provides the best balance, with conductivity that can be precisely controlled through doping, making it ideal for device fabrication.

Temperature Dependence

Semiconductors have a unique characteristic: their conductivity increases with temperature, unlike metals which show decreasing conductivity with increasing temperature.

Effects of Temperature Increase

1
Increased Thermal Energy

More electrons gain sufficient energy to jump energy bands

2
More Carriers

Increased number of electrons in conduction band and holes in valence band

3
Higher Conductivity

Net result is increased overall electrical conductivity

4
Temperature Coefficient

Negative coefficient - conductivity increases with temperature

Temperature Sensitivity Comparison

Germanium (Ge)

Very Sensitive to Temperature

  • Small band gap causes high sensitivity
  • Conductivity increases rapidly
  • High leakage at elevated temperatures
  • Limited temperature range
Silicon (Si)

Moderate Sensitivity

  • Balanced band gap (1.12 eV)
  • Reasonable temperature control
  • Good up to ~150°C
  • Industry standard for most applications
GaAs

More Stable at High Temps

  • Larger band gap (1.43 eV)
  • Better high-temperature performance
  • Lower leakage current
  • Ideal for extreme environments

Summary

Germanium is most sensitive to temperature and performs poorly at elevated temperatures.

Silicon provides good performance over a reasonable temperature range and is the industry standard.

Gallium Arsenide performs exceptionally well at high temperatures, making it the best choice for extreme environmental conditions.
