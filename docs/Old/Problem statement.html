Problem Statement

1. Silicon (Si): The Industry Workhorse Silicon remains the king of the industry, not because it’s the "fastest," but because it is the most reliable and easiest to manufacture.

  • Moderate Band Gap (1.12 eV): This "Goldilocks" energy gap allows it to operate reliably at room temperature without excessive electron leakage.
  • Thermal Stability: It can handle the heat generated by dense chips (like the CPU in your laptop) up to roughly 150°C.
  • Mature Ecosystem: Decades of "CMOS" fabrication refinement make it incredibly cost-effective for mass-producing large-scale integrated circuits.
  • The Limitation: It has lower carrier mobility than the others, meaning it struggles when forced into ultra-high-frequency (microwave) ranges.
2. Germanium (Ge): The Speed Specialist Germanium was the material used in the very first transistors, and it’s seeing a resurgence in specialized tech due to its raw conductivity.
  • High Carrier Mobility: Charge moves through Germanium much faster than Silicon, leading to superior electrical conductivity and faster switching.
  • Small Band Gap (0.66 eV): Because the gap is so small, it takes very little energy to move electrons.
  • The Thermal Trade-off: That same small band gap is its downfall; even at moderate temperatures, "intrinsic carriers" (unwanted electrons) jump the gap, causing massive leakage current and instability.
  • Key Use: High-sensitivity sensors and infrared (IR) optics.
3. Gallium Arsenide (GaAs): The Optoelectronic Elite
    GaAs is a III-V compound semiconductor (combining elements from groups III and V of the periodic table), giving it properties simple elements can’t match.
  • Direct Band Gap (1.42 eV): This is the "magic" property. In GaAs, electrons can emit a photon (light) directly when they drop energy levels. This makes it essential for LEDs and Lasers.
  • High Electron Mobility: It operates at speeds far beyond Silicon, making it the standard for 5G, satellite communications, and microwave applications.
  • The Barrier: It is significantly more expensive to manufacture and lacks a stable natural oxide like Silicon, making it harder to build complex logic gates.
