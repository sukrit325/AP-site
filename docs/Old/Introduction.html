Introduction

Semiconductors are materials with electrical conductivity between conductors and insulators. Their conductivity can be precisely controlled through temperature changes, doping (adding impurities), or applied electric fields, making them essential for modern electronics. They form the foundation of devices such as diodes, transistors, integrated circuits (ICs), solar cells, and communication systems. The most important semiconductor materials are Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), and Gallium Arsenide (GaAs). Each offers distinct properties:
  • Silicon widely used in ICs due to its stability, low cost, and mature fabrication technology
  • Germanium provides high carrier mobility
  • Gallium Arsenide excels in high-frequency and optoelectronic applications thanks to its direct band gap and superior electron mobility
Key properties that determine device performance include:
  • Band gap energy affects ease of electron excitation
  • Carrier mobility influences device speed
  • Conductivity determines current flow capability
  • Temperature behavior impacts reliability and efficiency
This project compares the fundamental physical and electrical properties of Silicon, Germanium, and Gallium Arsenide through theoretical analysis and simulation — helping explain why certain materials are preferred for specific applications.
