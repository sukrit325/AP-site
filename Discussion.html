The performance of semiconductor devices is fundamentally determined by intrinsic material properties such as bandgap energy, carrier mobility, thermal conductivity, and critical electric field strength. These characteristics define efficiency, switching capability, voltage tolerance, and thermal reliability. As modern electronic systems demand higher power density and faster switching speeds, selecting suitable semiconductor materials has become increasingly important. Wide bandgap semiconductors such as silicon carbide (SiC) and gallium nitride (GaN) demonstrate clear advantages in high-power and high-temperature applications. Their large bandgaps reduce leakage currents at elevated temperatures, improving efficiency and stability. SiC offers excellent thermal conductivity and high breakdown strength, making it suitable for electric vehicle inverters and high-voltage converters, although fabrication costs remain high. GaN provides high electron velocity and efficient high-frequency switching, supporting compact designs in RF systems and fast chargers, though thermal management and large-scale manufacturing remain challenges. Gallium arsenide (GaAs) delivers superior electron mobility for microwave and optoelectronic applications but is limited in high-power use due to cost and lower thermal conductivity. Silicon, while dominant due to its low cost and mature infrastructure, faces performance limitations under extreme conditions. Overall, no single material is universally optimal. Future semiconductor development will focus on advanced material engineering, heterostructures, and emerging two-dimensional materials to further enhance performance and efficiency.
