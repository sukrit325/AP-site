Problem Statement

The rapid growth of modern electronics, communication systems, renewable energy technologies, and high-speed computing has increased the demand for semiconductor materials with optimized electrical and thermal properties. Device performance is fundamentally governed by intrinsic characteristics such as band gap energy, carrier mobility, intrinsic carrier concentration, and temperature-dependent conductivity. Among the most important materials are Silicon, Germanium, and Gallium Arsenide, each offering distinct advantages and limitations. Silicon, with a moderate band gap (~1.12 eV), provides good thermal stability and benefits from highly mature fabrication technology, making it dominant in CMOS and large-scale integrated circuits. However, its relatively low carrier mobility limits high-frequency performance. Germanium has a smaller band gap (~0.66 eV) and higher carrier mobility, enabling faster charge transport, but its higher intrinsic carrier concentration leads to increased leakage current and reduced thermal stability at elevated temperatures. Gallium Arsenide, a direct band gap (~1.42 eV) III–V semiconductor, offers high electron mobility and efficient light emission, making it ideal for optoelectronic and microwave applications, though higher cost and fabrication complexity restrict widespread use. A major challenge in material selection lies in the trade-offs between mobility, conductivity, and thermal stability, all of which are temperature dependent. Therefore, a unified theoretical and simulation-based comparison is essential to evaluate performance under identical conditions and establish physics-based guidelines for material selection in advanced electronic applications.
