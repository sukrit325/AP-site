Methodology

1. Introduction to Semiconductor Materials

Semiconductors are materials whose electrical conductivity lies between conductors and insulators. Their conductivity can be controlled by temperature, electric field, and impurity doping. This unique property makes semiconductors essential for electronic devices such as diodes, transistors, solar cells, and integrated circuits. The electrical behavior of semiconductors is explained using band theory of solids, which describes the energy levels available to electrons. There are two important energy bands: Valence band: occupied by electrons Conduction band: where electrons move freely and conduct electricity The energy difference between these bands is called the band gap (Eg). If electrons gain sufficient energy (thermal or optical), they jump from the valence band to the conduction band, leaving behind holes. Both electrons and holes act as charge carriers.

2. Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductor A pure semiconductor is called intrinsic semiconductor. Examples include Silicon (Si) and Germanium (Ge). In intrinsic semiconductors: Number of electrons = number of holes Conductivity depends mainly on temperature Extrinsic Semiconductor When impurities are added intentionally, the semiconductor becomes extrinsic. This process is called doping. Two types: n-type: excess electrons (donor impurity) p-type: excess holes (acceptor impurity) Doping increases conductivity significantly.

3. Band Gap Energy

Band gap is the most important property that determines semiconductor performance. Small band gap → higher conductivity → more leakage current Large band gap → lower leakage → better high temperature performance Typical band gap values: Silicon (Si): 1.12 eV Germanium (Ge): 0.66 eV Gallium Arsenide (GaAs): 1.43 eV Germanium has the smallest band gap, so it conducts easily but is unstable at high temperatures. Gallium Arsenide has a larger band gap, making it suitable for high-frequency and optoelectronic applications.

4. Charge Carrier Mobility

Mobility refers to how fast electrons and holes move when an electric field is applied. Where μ = mobility vd = drift velocity E = electric field Higher mobility results in: Faster switching devices Better performance at high frequency Typical electron mobility: Silicon: 1350 cm²/V·s Germanium: 3900 cm²/V·s Gallium Arsenide: 8500 cm²/V·s GaAs has the highest mobility, making it ideal for microwave and high-speed applications.

5. Electrical Conductivity

Electrical conductivity depends on charge carrier concentration and mobility. Where: σ = conductivity q = charge of electron n = electron concentration p = hole concentration μn, μp = mobilities Higher mobility and carrier concentration result in higher conductivity. Germanium has higher conductivity than Silicon due to smaller band gap. GaAs also shows high conductivity due to very high electron mobility.

6. Temperature Dependence

Semiconductors have negative temperature coefficient, meaning conductivity increases with temperature. As temperature increases: More electrons gain energy More electrons move to conduction band Conductivity increases Germanium is more sensitive to temperature changes due to its small band gap. Gallium Arsenide performs better at high temperatures compared to Silicon and Germanium.
